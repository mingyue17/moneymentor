Investing basics

The simple version: saving protects cash you need soon. Investing is for money that can stay put long enough to handle ups and downs.

The three ideas that matter first
  • Time beats timing. Nobody reliably picks the perfect day. A steady habit matters more.
  • Compounding. Returns can earn more returns, so starting small and early is powerful.
  • Emergency cash comes first. Keep 3-6 months of expenses before taking market risk.
Why investing exists

Prices rise over time. Cash in a basic savings account can slowly lose buying power. Investing is the tool people use to try to keep up with inflation and grow money over years.

Example: S$1,000 growing at 5% a year for 10 years becomes about S$1,630. The number is only an illustration, not a promise.

How small can a start be?

In Singapore, Singapore Savings Bonds start from S$500, while some regular ETF savings plans start around S$100 a month. The right starting point depends on your horizon and risk comfort.

Risk and returns

The simple version: higher possible return usually means a higher chance of loss. High return with no risk is a red flag.

The beginner risk ladder
  • SSBs and T-bills: government-backed, lower return, best for short horizons.
  • Bond funds: usually calmer than stocks, but prices still move.
  • Broad ETFs: diversified, better suited to longer horizons.
  • Individual stocks and REITs: more homework, more single-company risk.
  • Crypto, leverage, guaranteed schemes: not a beginner foundation.
How to choose your spot

Ask: when do I need this money back, and could I sleep if it dropped 20%? Short horizon or high anxiety points to safer products. Five-plus years and calm risk comfort can make broad ETFs reasonable for part of the money.

The one rule worth remembering

If you cannot explain what could go wrong with an investment, you do not understand it well enough to buy it yet.

Before sending real money, check the firm on the MAS Investor Alert List and the MAS Financial Institutions Directory.
