Investing basics

Why bother investing at all? Try it.

Investing means putting money into something — a company, a fund, a bond — expecting it to be worth more later. Words are boring; drag the slider and watch what actually happens to your money.

Illustration only — 5% is a long-term average for diversified investing, not a promise. Real returns swing up and down year to year, which is exactly what the next section shows.

The three rules that matter: start early (compounding needs time), only invest money you won’t need for 5 years, and keep 3–6 months of expenses as emergency savings first. In Singapore you can start from S$100/month.
Risk and returns

What S$1,000 could look like after one year.

Every investment trades safety for growth. Here’s the same S$1,000 on each rung of the ladder, in a typical good year and a typical bad year — so you can feel the difference, not just read about it.

Very low risk

Government-backed savings

Singapore Savings Bonds and T-bills. The Government guarantees your principal — even in a terrible market year, you don’t lose.

e.g. SSB from S$500 · T-bills from S$1,000

Low risk

Bond funds

You lend money to governments and companies through one fund. Calmer than stocks, but prices dip when interest rates rise.

Moderate risk

Broad market ETFs

One fund holding many companies — the STI ETF holds Singapore’s top 30. Falls hurt, but 29 other companies cushion any single disaster. This is the classic long-term core.

e.g. STI ETF (ES3/G3B) · from ~S$100/month

High risk

One single stock

All your eggs in one company. If DBS has a great year, you win big. If your one company stumbles — scandal, bad earnings, lawsuit — nothing cushions you.

e.g. one counter only: DBS, Singtel, a REIT...

Very high risk

Crypto, leverage & hype coins

Prices driven by hype, not earnings. Doubling is possible; losing almost everything is just as possible — and “guaranteed win” versions are outright scams.

Illustrative ranges based on how these asset types have historically behaved — not predictions, not guarantees. Any single year can be better or worse.

How to pick your rung: ask when you need the money back, and how you’d feel if it dropped 20% next month. Need it soon or would lose sleep → stay low. 5+ years and a calm stomach → broad ETFs make sense for part of it.
